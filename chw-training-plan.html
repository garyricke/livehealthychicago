Module 1Understanding High Blood Pressure6 minutes

Gary

Hello everyone, and welcome! My name is Alex, and I'll be your host today. Thank you for joining us for this hypertension education training designed specifically for Community Health Workers. Every day, Community Health Workers help people understand their health, connect with healthcare services, and make informed decisions. Your role is essential because you are often the trusted link between the healthcare system and the community. Today, we'll talk about one of the most common health conditions affecting adults—high blood pressure, also called hypertension. Joining me is Ella, a registered nurse and nurse educator. Ella, thank you for being here.

Rework the open — it introduces the AI host “Alex.” Instead, Gary gives a short warm welcome, or we open straight on Ella. No AI narrator.
Ella

Thank you, Alex. And thank you to each of you for being here today. High blood pressure affects millions of adults and is one of the leading risk factors for heart disease and stroke. The good news is that it can often be prevented, managed, and treated. As Community Health Workers, you have an important opportunity to help people understand their blood pressure, recognize their risks, and make healthier choices before serious complications develop. Let's begin with the basics.

Gary

People hear the words blood pressure all the time, but many aren't sure what it actually means. How do you explain blood pressure in a simple way?

Ella

One way I explain it is by using a garden hose. Imagine you're watering your garden. When the water pressure is just right, the water flows smoothly through the hose. But if the pressure stays too high every day, the hose begins to stretch and weaken. Our blood vessels work in a very similar way. The American Heart Association describes blood pressure as the force of blood pushing against the walls of the arteries as the heart pumps blood throughout the body. That's a medical definition, but I like to simplify it. Blood pressure is simply the pressure your blood uses to move through your blood vessels and deliver oxygen and nutrients to every part of your body. We actually need blood pressure. The goal isn't to have low blood pressure or no blood pressure. The goal is to have healthy blood pressure.

Gary

People also hear the word hypertension. Is that different from high blood pressure?

Ella

No. Hypertension is simply the medical term for high blood pressure. It means the pressure inside the arteries stays higher than it should over time. One of the biggest challenges is that most people don't feel sick. Someone can have high blood pressure for months—or even years—without noticing any symptoms. That's why regular blood pressure checks are so important. Finding high blood pressure early gives people the chance to manage it before it causes serious health problems.

Gary

If people don't usually feel sick, why is high blood pressure such a concern?

Ella

I like to compare it to a small leak hidden behind the wall of a house. At first, everything looks normal. You don't see the leak. You don't hear it. But over time, that small leak can quietly weaken the wall and cause major damage before anyone realizes there's a problem. High blood pressure works much the same way. Even when someone feels perfectly healthy, uncontrolled high blood pressure can slowly damage the body. Over time, it can affect the heart, increasing the risk of heart attack and heart failure. It can damage the brain, increasing the risk of stroke and problems with memory and thinking. It can affect the kidneys, making it harder for them to filter waste from the blood. It can damage the eyes, leading to vision problems or even vision loss. It can also damage the blood vessels, making it harder for blood to reach important organs. The encouraging news is that many of these complications can be prevented. The American Heart Association emphasizes that knowing your blood pressure, working with a healthcare provider, taking medications as prescribed when needed, and making healthy lifestyle changes can greatly reduce these risks.

Let an on-screen graphic carry the organ-by-organ complications while Ella names two or three out loud — trims the runtime.
Gary

Does high blood pressure affect everyone the same way?

Ella

High blood pressure can affect anyone. Men and women. Young adults and older adults. People from every race and ethnicity. However, some people have a greater chance of developing high blood pressure than others. We call these risk factors. They fall into two groups. Some risk factors cannot be changed, while others can be changed. Understanding the difference helps Community Health Workers know who may benefit from earlier screening and where we can help reduce risk.

Gary

Let's start with the risk factors we can't change. What are they?

Ella

Some risk factors are simply part of who we are. These include getting older, having a family history of high blood pressure, and certain medical conditions, such as diabetes or chronic kidney disease. We also know that high blood pressure affects some populations more than others. For example, African American adults are more likely to develop high blood pressure at younger ages and experience more severe complications. This isn't caused by one single factor. It's influenced by genetics, chronic stress, access to healthcare, economic conditions, and other social factors that affect health. The important message is this: Having one or more of these risk factors does not mean someone will definitely develop high blood pressure. It simply means it's even more important to know their numbers, have regular blood pressure checks, and focus on the things they can control.

Gary

And what are the risk factors people can change?

Ella

This is the encouraging part of the conversation. Many of the things that influence blood pressure are things we can improve over time. These include:

  • Eating a heart-healthy diet.
  • Reducing sodium.
  • Being physically active.
  • Managing stress.
  • Getting enough sleep.
  • Avoiding tobacco products.
  • Limiting alcohol.
  • Taking medications as prescribed.
  • Keeping regular healthcare appointments.

The good news is that people don't have to change everything at once. Research consistently shows that small, consistent changes are more likely to become lasting habits than trying to change everything overnight. As Community Health Workers, our role is to encourage progress—not perfection.

The graphic can show this full list; Ella speaks a few examples. Same move keeps every read-aloud list shorter.
Gary

Before we move to the next section, what's the one message you'd like every Community Health Worker to remember?

Ella

I'd like them to remember this: High blood pressure is common, but it doesn't have to become dangerous. When people know their numbers, understand their risks, and receive the right support, they can take meaningful steps to protect their health. And as Community Health Workers, you have the privilege of helping people take those first steps.

Module 2Preventing and Managing High Blood Pressure9 minutes

Gary

Ella, now that we understand what high blood pressure is and who is at risk, let's talk about what people can do. If someone has just been told they have high blood pressure, what's one of the first conversations you have with them?

Ella

One of the first things we talk about is lifestyle. The good news is that many of the things that help lower blood pressure are things people can begin working on today. Research and recommendations from organizations like the American Heart Association consistently show that healthy eating, regular physical activity, managing stress, getting enough sleep, and taking medications as prescribed all work together to improve blood pressure and reduce the risk of heart disease and stroke. The important thing to remember is this: People don't have to change everything at once. Small, consistent changes can make a big difference over time. Nutrition

Gary

One of the first things people hear is, "You need to eat less salt." But many people don't really know what that means. How do you explain it?

Ella

That's one of the questions I hear most often. The American Heart Association recommends no more than 2,300 milligrams of sodium each day, and for many adults with high blood pressure, an ideal goal is 1,500 milligrams per day. Those numbers don't mean much to most people. So I like to make them visual. Imagine holding one teaspoon of table salt in your hand. That one teaspoon contains about 2,300 milligrams of sodium. That's the maximum amount most adults should have in an entire day. Not one meal. The whole day. Most people are surprised by that. For example, a fast-food breakfast sandwich alone can contain about half of that daily amount. By lunchtime, after a deli sandwich and chips, many people have already gone over the recommended limit. One thing I always remind people is that most of the sodium we eat doesn't come from the salt shaker. It comes from packaged foods, canned foods, frozen meals, restaurant meals, and fast food. Once people know where sodium is hiding, they can begin making healthier choices.

Gary

So does that mean people have to stop eating the foods they grew up with?

Ella

Absolutely not. Healthy eating doesn't mean giving up your culture. Food is more than nutrition. It's family. It's tradition. It's comfort. Our goal isn't to change cultural foods. Our goal is to help people prepare those foods in healthier ways. For example, with traditional soul food, we might suggest baking or air-frying chicken more often instead of frying it. Instead of seasoning collard greens with ham hocks, try smoked turkey. Use herbs and spices like garlic, onions, paprika, thyme, oregano, rosemary, black pepper, or cayenne to add flavor while using less salt. Many traditional foods are already heart healthy, including black-eyed peas, beans, sweet potatoes, and leafy greens. The same idea applies to Latino families. Beans, avocados, fresh salsa, fruits, vegetables, and corn tortillas can all be part of a heart-healthy eating pattern. Simple changes, like adding more vegetables, choosing smaller portions of rice, or baking plantains instead of frying them, can make a meaningful difference. Healthy eating isn't about changing someone's culture. It's about making healthier choices while honoring the foods and traditions that matter most.

The “not about changing your culture” point lands twice in the nutrition section — keep the stronger version.
Gary

Those are wonderful ideas. But what if someone says, "I'd love to eat healthier, but I don't have a grocery store nearby, I don't have transportation, or I don't have time to cook."

Ella

Those are very real challenges, and many of the people we serve face them every day. As Community Health Workers, one of the most important things we can do is listen before offering advice. Instead of asking, "Why aren't you eating healthier?" I ask, "What's making healthy eating difficult for you?" That one question changes the conversation. Once we understand the barrier, we can help people find realistic solutions. Frozen vegetables are just as nutritious as fresh vegetables and often cost less. If canned vegetables or beans are all that's available, choosing low-sodium options—or rinsing them before eating—can reduce sodium. For someone working long hours, a rotisserie chicken, frozen vegetables, and microwave brown rice can make a quick, balanced meal. Healthy eating doesn't have to be perfect. It has to be practical. Physical Activity

Gary

Besides healthy eating, what else can people do?

Ella

One of the best things people can do is move their body. I actually prefer that phrase over "exercise," because movement looks different for everyone. The goal isn't to become an athlete. The goal is simply to move a little more than yesterday.

Gary

What does "moving your body" look like?

Ella

It can be walking around the neighborhood. Taking the stairs. Gardening. Dancing to your favorite music. Playing with your children or grandchildren. Stretching while watching television. Or doing chair exercises if walking is difficult. The American Heart Association recommends about 150 minutes of moderate physical activity each week, but that doesn't have to happen all at once. Three 10-minute walks count. Two 15-minute walks count. Every minute of movement counts.

Gary

What if someone says, "I work two jobs. I don't have time to exercise."

Ella

I hear that often. Instead of asking someone to completely change their schedule, I ask, "Where could you add just five or ten minutes of movement to your day?" Maybe it's walking during a break. Maybe it's dancing while cooking dinner. Maybe it's marching in place during a commercial break. The goal isn't to find more time. It's to make the most of the time you already have. I always remind people: Movement is medicine. Stress, Sleep, and Medications

Gary

We've talked about food and physical activity. Are there other lifestyle habits that affect blood pressure?

Ella

Absolutely. Stress, sleep, and taking medications as prescribed all play an important role. When we're under constant stress, our bodies release hormones that can temporarily raise blood pressure. Over time, stress can also make it harder to eat well, stay active, sleep enough, or remember to take medications. Most adults should aim for 7 to 9 hours of sleep each night. Poor sleep—and conditions like sleep apnea—can make blood pressure harder to control. And finally, medications. One of the things I hear most often is, "I feel fine, so I stopped taking my medicine." Blood pressure medicines help control high blood pressure—they don't cure it. If someone has trouble taking their medication because of side effects, cost, or another concern, encourage them to talk with their healthcare provider or pharmacist before stopping it. There may be other options that work better.

Gary

Before we move on, what's the one message you'd like every Community Health Worker to remember from this section?

Ella

I'd like them to remember this: Healthy habits don't have to be perfect. They have to be realistic, consistent, and sustainable. As Community Health Workers, our role isn't to expect perfection. Our role is to help people identify one healthy change they can start today. Because small, consistent choices can lead to healthier hearts and healthier lives.

This closer echoes Module 1’s “small, consistent changes.” Vary one so the module endings don’t repeat.

Module 3Know Your Numbers8–9 minutes

Gary

Ella, we've talked about why high blood pressure matters and what people can do to lower their risk. One thing people often tell me is, "I got my blood pressure checked, but I have no idea what those two numbers mean." Can you explain them?

Ella

Absolutely. Whenever blood pressure is measured, you'll see two numbers. The top number is called the systolic blood pressure. That's the pressure inside your arteries when your heart squeezes to pump blood throughout your body. The bottom number is called the diastolic blood pressure. That's the pressure when your heart relaxes between beats. One simple way I explain it is by clapping my hands. When my hands come together, that's like the heart squeezing. When they separate, that's like the heart relaxing before the next beat. Both numbers are important because together they tell us how hard the heart is working.

Gary

Now that we know what those numbers represent, how do we know if they're normal or high?

Ella

That's where the American Heart Association blood pressure categories can help. These categories help healthcare providers determine whether a person's blood pressure is in a healthy range or whether they need closer monitoring or treatment. As Community Health Workers, your role isn't to diagnose anyone. Your role is to recognize when someone should be encouraged to follow up with their healthcare provider and when a reading may require immediate attention.

On-screen graphic — Category Blood Pressure · Normal · Less than 120 AND less than 80 · Elevated · 120–129 AND less than 80 · Stage 1 Hypertension · 130–139 OR 80–89 · Stage 2 Hypertension · 140 or higher OR 90 or higher · Hypertensive Crisis · Higher than 180 AND/OR higher than 120
Ella

Don't worry about memorizing every number today. Instead, remember these three points: A normal blood pressure is what we're aiming for. If the blood pressure stays high over time, the person should follow up with their healthcare provider. And if it's extremely high, it may require immediate medical attention—which we'll discuss in our next module.

Gary

That makes sense. But for those numbers to be useful, the blood pressure has to be measured correctly. How important is proper technique?

Ella

It's extremely important. A blood pressure reading helps guide healthcare decisions, but it's only useful if it's accurate. Even small things—like drinking coffee right before the test, talking during the measurement, crossing your legs, or using the wrong cuff size—can affect the reading. That's why the American Heart Association recommends following a few simple steps before, during, and after measuring blood pressure. BEFORE

Gary

Let's start with before taking the blood pressure. What should people do?

Ella

Before checking blood pressure:

  • Avoid smoking, caffeine, or exercise for about 30 minutes, because these can temporarily raise blood pressure.
  • Empty your bladder if needed.
  • Sit quietly and rest for at least five minutes before taking the reading.

Those few minutes allow your body to return to its resting state and help produce a more accurate reading.

On-screen graphic — BEFORE YOU CHECK Rest for 5 minutes · No smoking for 30 minutes · No caffeine for 30 minutes · No exercise for 30 minutes · Empty your bladder · DURING
Gary

Once someone is ready, what's the best way to take the blood pressure?

Ella

During the measurement:

  • Sit in a chair with your back supported.
  • Keep both feet flat on the floor and avoid crossing your legs.
  • Rest your arm on a table so the cuff is at heart level.
  • Place the cuff on a bare arm, not over clothing, because clothing can affect the reading.
  • Use the correct cuff size, since a cuff that's too small or too large can produce an inaccurate reading.
  • Finally, try not to talk during the measurement. Even talking can temporarily change the reading.
On-screen graphic — DURING THE MEASUREMENT Back supported · Feet flat · Legs uncrossed · Arm at heart level · Bare arm · Correct cuff size · Don't talk · AFTER
Gary

Once the reading is finished, what should people do?

Ella

If they're checking their blood pressure at home, encourage them to record the reading in a notebook, a phone app, or the monitor's memory. One reading gives us a snapshot. Several readings over time tell a much more complete story. Bringing those readings to healthcare appointments helps providers make informed treatment decisions.

Gary

Those sound like small details. Do they really make that much of a difference?

Ella

They absolutely do. These simple steps may seem small, but together they help ensure the reading truly reflects a person's blood pressure. An accurate blood pressure reading gives people—and their healthcare providers—the information they need to make the best decisions about their health.

Gary

Before we finish this section, what's the one message you'd like every Community Health Worker to remember?

Ella

I'd like them to remember this: Knowing your numbers is important, but those numbers are only helpful if they're accurate. By following a few simple steps before, during, and after measuring blood pressure, Community Health Workers can help ensure readings are reliable and support better healthcare decisions.

Module 4Recognizing a Blood Pressure Emergency4–5 minutes

Gary

Ella, we've talked about how to measure blood pressure and what the numbers mean. Now let's talk about something every Community Health Worker should know. When does a high blood pressure reading become an emergency?

Ella

That's an important question because not every high blood pressure reading is an emergency. The American Heart Association explains that a blood pressure reading higher than 180 over 120 needs immediate attention, but the next step depends on whether the person has symptoms. The first thing to do is stay calm. wait at least one minute and then check the blood pressure again. Sometimes a second reading is lower if the person has had a chance to rest. If the second reading is still above 180 over 120, the next step is to check for symptoms.

Gary

What symptoms should Community Health Workers look for?

Ella

The American Heart Association recommends watching for symptoms such as:

  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Back pain
  • Numbness
  • Weakness
  • Changes in vision
  • Difficulty speaking

These symptoms may mean the person's organs are not getting enough blood flow and they need emergency medical care.

On-screen graphic — Blood Pressure Higher Than 180/120? Step 1 · Wait 1 minute · Check again · Step 2 · Look for symptoms: · Chest pain · Shortness of breath · Back pain · Numbness · Weakness · Vision changes · Difficulty speaking
Gary

What if someone has a blood pressure over 180 over 120 but doesn't have any of those symptoms?

Ella

According to the American Heart Association, this is often called severe hypertension. It usually does not require calling 911, but it does require prompt medical attention. The person should contact their healthcare provider as soon as possible because their medications or treatment plan may need to be adjusted. As Community Health Workers, we do not diagnose or change medications. Your role is to encourage the person to contact their healthcare provider promptly and or help them contact their provider.

Gary

And what if they do have symptoms?

Ella

If the blood pressure is higher than 180 over 120 and the person has symptoms like chest pain, trouble breathing, weakness, numbness, vision changes, or difficulty speaking, call 911 immediately. Don't wait to see if the blood pressure comes down on its own. This may be a hypertensive emergency, and every minute matters.

Gary

Some of those symptoms sound a lot like a stroke. Is there an easy way to remember them?

Ella

Yes. One of the easiest ways is the BE FAST acronym. It helps us recognize the warning signs of a stroke quickly.

On-screen graphic — BE FAST B – Balance · Sudden loss of balance or coordination · E – Eyes · Sudden vision changes · F – Face · Does one side of the face droop? · A – Arms · Does one arm drift downward? · S – Speech · Is speech slurred or difficult to understand? · T – Time · Time to call 911 immediately.
Ella

The most important letter is T. Time. The sooner someone receives treatment for a stroke, the better their chance of recovery. If you think someone is having a stroke, don't drive them to the hospital yourself unless that's the only option. Call 911 so emergency medical professionals can begin treatment as quickly as possible.

Gary

Before we finish, what's the one message you'd like Community Health Workers to remember?

Ella

I'd like them to remember this: High blood pressure doesn't always mean an emergency—but it should never be ignored. If the blood pressure is over 180 over 120, check it again after one minute. If symptoms are present, call 911 immediately. If there are no symptoms, encourage the person to contact their healthcare provider as soon as possible. Knowing what to do in those first few minutes can make a life-saving difference.

Module 5The Community Health Worker's Role: Turning Knowledge into Action4–5 minutes

Gary

Ella, we've covered a lot of information today. We've talked about high blood pressure, healthy lifestyle changes, how to measure blood pressure correctly, and when someone needs emergency medical attention. But knowing the information is only part of the job. How do Community Health Workers help people actually make changes?

Ella

That's a wonderful question. Community Health Workers do much more than share information. You build relationships. You earn trust. You help people feel heard. Sometimes the most powerful thing you can do isn't giving advice. It's listening. People are much more likely to make changes when they feel respected and understood.

Gary

Can you give us an example?

Ella

Absolutely. Instead of asking, "Why aren't you taking your medication?" try asking, "Can you tell me what's making it difficult to take your medication?" Maybe they can't afford it. Maybe they're having side effects. Maybe they forgot. Maybe they don't understand why they need it. The answer changes how we can help. The same is true with healthy eating. Instead of saying, "You need to eat healthier," ask, "What makes healthy eating difficult for you?" One simple question can completely change the conversation.

Gary

Once you've explained something, how do you know the person understood?

Ella

One of my favorite tools is called the teach-back method. Instead of asking, "Do you understand?" I ask, "Just so I know I explained it clearly, can you tell me in your own words what you'll do when you get home?" Teach-back isn't a test. It's a way for me to make sure I explained it clearly. If someone can't explain it back to me, I simply try a different way.

Gary

Sometimes people feel overwhelmed. They've been told to eat healthier, exercise more, reduce stress, sleep better, lose weight, and take medication. Where should they begin?

Ella

I always encourage people to begin with one realistic goal. Trying to change everything at once can feel overwhelming. Instead, choose one step. Maybe it's replacing one sugary drink with water. Maybe it's walking for ten minutes after dinner three days this week. Maybe it's checking blood pressure twice this week. Small goals build confidence. Confidence helps people believe they can succeed. And success often leads to the next healthy habit.

Gary

What if they don't meet that goal?

Ella

That's okay. Healthy behavior change isn't always a straight line. Instead of asking, "Why didn't you do it?" ask, "What got in the way?" Then work together to adjust the plan. Our goal isn't perfection. Our goal is progress.

Gary

When should a Community Health Worker encourage someone to see a healthcare provider?

Ella

Community Health Workers are educators and trusted partners, but we don't diagnose medical conditions or change treatment plans. Encourage someone to contact their healthcare provider if:

  • Their blood pressure readings stay high over time.
  • They have questions about their medications.
  • They're experiencing side effects.
  • They develop new symptoms.
  • They need help managing other health conditions.

And remember, if someone has a blood pressure over 180/120 with symptoms like chest pain, shortness of breath, weakness, vision changes, or difficulty speaking, call 911 immediately.

Gary

Before we finish today's training, what's the one message you'd like every Community Health Worker to remember?

Ella

I'd like to leave you with this. Knowledge is important. But how we share that knowledge matters just as much. When people feel respected... they're more willing to listen. When they feel heard... they're more willing to change. And when they believe someone cares about them... they're more likely to take that first step toward better health. Never underestimate the power of a conversation. One blood pressure check. One healthier meal. One short walk. One medication taken as prescribed. One question asked with kindness. One person who finally feels heard. Those moments change lives. Thank you for the work you do every day. Together, we're helping build healthier communities—one conversation at a time.

Gary

Ella, before we wrap up, we've shared a lot of information today. What resources are available if Community Health Workers or community members want to continue learning about high blood pressure?

Ella

I'm so glad you asked because one of the most important things Community Health Workers can do is connect people with reliable, evidence-based resources. You don't have to memorize everything we discussed today, and you don't have to have every answer. Knowing where to find trusted information is just as important. As part of this toolkit, you'll receive a Live Healthy Chicago educational pamphlet that includes QR codes linking you to additional resources. These resources can help you and the people you serve learn more about:

  • Understanding high blood pressure
  • Healthy eating and reducing sodium
  • Physical activity
  • Home blood pressure monitoring
  • Community programs and local resources
  • Information from trusted organizations like the American Heart Association

I encourage you to explore these resources and share them with the people you serve. Sometimes, giving someone the right resource is the first step toward helping them take control of their health.

Gary

That's wonderful because it means today's training doesn't end here.

Ella

Exactly. Learning is an ongoing journey, and Community Health Workers don't have to do it alone. The QR codes in your pamphlet are there to support you long after today's training is over. Whether you need a quick refresher, educational materials to share with community members, or additional information about high blood pressure, these resources are available whenever you need them.
